# Module 1.2: Oranges

## The Orange

*One of the most widely recognised citrus fruits, known for its bright colour and balanced sweet–tart flavour.*

Welcome to Module 1. This module explores how fruits can be carefully observed, described, and organised into a simple catalogue format. Citrus fruits are particularly useful for this kind of practice because they combine vivid colour, distinctive scent, varied textures, and complex flavours. The orange provides an excellent starting point.

Although oranges are familiar to most people, careful observation reveals many details that often go unnoticed. The outer peel is thick and slightly dimpled, with tiny oil glands that release a strong citrus fragrance when the skin is scratched or torn. When peeled, the fruit separates into distinct segments, each wrapped in delicate membranes that hold the juicy pulp inside.

Oranges belong to the genus *Citrus*, which also includes lemons, limes, mandarins, and grapefruits.@delgado2022citrus These fruits share structural similarities—such as segmented interiors and aromatic skins—but they differ significantly in flavour and appearance. Compared with many other citrus fruits, oranges are notable for their balance of sweetness and acidity, which makes them widely consumed as fresh fruit and juice.

The orange also demonstrates how visual and sensory cues shape our expectations of food. Its bright orange colour often signals ripeness and sweetness, while the scent released from the peel hints at the refreshing flavour inside. When eaten, the fruit’s juiciness and balanced taste contribute to the familiar citrus flavour profile associated with oranges.@harper2021sensory

In this module you will learn how to organise fruit observations into short catalogue entries. A catalogue entry typically combines a small image with a concise descriptive paragraph that highlights the most noticeable qualities of the fruit. This format is commonly used in field guides, botanical references, and food catalogues because it allows readers to quickly understand both the appearance and character of an item.@marshall2020catalogue

You will also continue thinking about how images and written descriptions support one another. A photograph communicates visual qualities such as colour, shape, and texture, while written descriptions can capture sensory experiences—such as aroma, flavour, or texture—that images alone cannot fully convey.@bogus

By the end of this module you should feel more confident organising your fruit observations into structured catalogue entries that combine descriptive writing and images. These skills will support your progress toward **Assessment 1**, where you will create a short fruit catalogue featuring three fruits of your choosing.
